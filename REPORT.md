# Sunflower Processing in East Carroll Parish & the Louisiana Delta
## Feasibility Research Report

**Prepared for:** Tap Parker Farms (William) — Lake Providence, LA area
**Date:** July 20, 2026
**Method:** Multi-agent deep research — 22 sources fetched, 76 claims extracted, 25 top claims adversarially verified (3-vote panel each): 20 confirmed, 5 refuted. Refuted claims are excluded from the findings below and listed at the end.

---

## Executive summary

Sunflowers grow well in the northeast Louisiana Delta, but **the nearest processing infrastructure is genuinely far away**: the main U.S. crush plants are in North Dakota (ADM Enderlin, Cargill West Fargo) and Lamar, Colorado, and the nearest large birdseed/wildlife-food processor is Red River Commodities in **Lubbock, TX (~600 road miles from Lake Providence)**. That means any local venture is a **market-creation play**, not a plug-into-an-existing-market play — which is both the risk and the opportunity ("huge untapped market" is directionally right: nobody serves this region).

Current economics look workable on paper — 2026 new-crop high-oleic contracts were bid around **$22.70–$22.80/cwt** in mid-October 2025, and USDA South Central grain truck rates (~$3.02/mi at 200-mile hauls) allow real freight modeling — but prices are volatile (oil-type seed nearly doubled from $16.60 to $31.40/cwt in the 2021 drought) and were expected to soften through the 2025/26 harvest.

**The two most credible entry paths:**

1. **Birdseed/wildlife-seed cleaning and bagging line** — turnkey domestic suppliers exist (e.g., MMCTECH, Charlotte NC); a birdseed-only line omits the expensive dehull/polish stages of a confection line and is materially cheaper in scope.
2. **Elevator partnership** — proven model where buyers exist; the technical hurdles (drying to 10% moisture, fines/fire risk) are well understood and manageable per NDSU extension guidance.

**Biggest gap:** every specific turnkey equipment price found online was **refuted in verification** (unreliable vendor figures), so **direct vendor quotes are the critical next step** before any break-even acreage math is trustworthy.

## 1. Existing infrastructure — who buys sunflowers today, and where

**Confidence: HIGH (verified 3-0)**

- No sunflower processing infrastructure exists near the Louisiana Delta.
- Main U.S. crush plants: **ADM Enderlin, ND; Cargill West Fargo, ND; Lamar, CO**.
- Nearest confection/roasting: **CHS, Fargo, ND** (kernel and inshell roasting).
- **Red River Commodities' nearest facility: Lubbock, TX (wildlife food), ~550–650 road miles** from Lake Providence. Their main plant (Fargo, ND) is 1,100+ miles. Lubbock is the closest plausible freight destination for birdseed-grade seed.
- The **National Sunflower Association buyer directory** (sunflowernsa.com/buyers) is the primary tool for finding specific buyers — 15 categories including Bird Food, Confection Processors, and Oilseed Crushers.

**Sources:** AgMRC sunflower profile; redriv.com/company/facilities; chsinc.com oilseed products; Farm & Ranch Guide 2025 crush contracts; sunflowernsa.com/buyers.

## 3. Market prices and volatility

**Confidence: MEDIUM (trade press, time-bound figures)**

- Mid-October 2025 crusher bids for **2026 new-crop high-oleic: ~$22.70–$22.80/cwt cash** ($22.20–$23 with act-of-God clause), with a 2% oil premium per point above 40% oil content.
- Prices were expected to **soften through 2025/26 harvest delivery**.
- Volatility is real and cuts both ways: the 2021 drought nearly **doubled** oil-type seed from $16.60 to $31.40/cwt — risk for a processor buying seed, upside for a grower-owned operation.
- Confection sunflower has historically commanded a **$3.50–$6.00/cwt premium** over oilseed (NSA pricing page; extracted but not among the top-verified claims — treat as directional).

**Sources:** Farm Progress (NSA crusher bids, USDA stocks); Audubon (NDSU economist Frayne Olson on 2021 prices).

## 4. The elevator-partnership model

**Confidence: MEDIUM (structural mechanisms hold; cost figures dated)**

- Where buyers exist, elevators profitably handle sunflower by **arbitraging bids** among crushers, exporters, and birdseed packagers, selling to the highest net of freight and handling (documented in western Kansas by K-State Extension, 1997).
- Handling costs scale strongly with throughput: **~$0.27/bu at 1.75M bu/yr down to ~$0.07/bu at 17.5M bu/yr** (OSU Extension); a 1991 wheat-elevator survey found $0.19–$0.22/bu. Both are dated — inflation-adjust and treat as shape-of-curve, not exact numbers.
- **Key caveat:** the model presupposes reachable buyers. A Delta elevator would likely sell to **one distant buyer** (e.g., Lubbock birdseed) rather than arbitrage several — a weaker position. Getting a contract commitment from Red River (or similar) **before** retrofitting is the right sequencing.

### Technical requirements for an elevator to handle sunflower — **Confidence: HIGH**

- Dry oil sunflower to **~10% moisture** for winter/short-term storage, **7–8%** for storage into summer (confection: 11%/10%). Louisiana's warmer climate makes these conservative minimums.
- **Fire risk comes from fines** — fine seed hairs/fibers rubbed off in handling ignite in the dryer fan and open burner. NDSU (Hellevang): "the fire hazard is not related to drying temperature, but rather to housekeeping and managing the dryer." Daily dryer cleaning is the control, not exotic equipment.
- Net: a standard Delta grain elevator can handle sunflower with **procedure changes and housekeeping discipline** more than heavy capital retrofit; the real costs are drying capacity, segregated storage, and dedicated handling to control fines.

**Sources:** K-State "Sunflower Marketing in the High Plains"; OSU Extension elevator cost study; NDSU Extension drying guidance; WinField United.

## 5. Processing equipment and startup scope

**Confidence: MEDIUM (line composition verified; ALL pricing refuted)**

- **MMCTECH Inc (Charlotte, NC)** verified as a real domestic supplier of scalable turnkey sunflower cleaning/dehulling plants (possibly imported/rebranded equipment).
- A full confection/kernel line comprises ~a dozen stages: bucket elevators, vibratory cleaner, aspirator, destoner, gravity separator, dehuller, grader, color sorter, polisher, dust collector, bagging.
- A **birdseed-only line omits the dehull/polish/color-sort stages** and is materially cheaper in scope — clean, screen, aspirate, bag.
- **No online equipment price survived verification.** ABC Machinery's "$5,500 for a 5 t/day press line" and refinery add-on figures were refuted 0-3 and 1-2; the common "~40% oil yield" rule of thumb was also refuted 0-3. **Do not build a budget on internet prices — get written quotes.**

## 6. Grants and financing

**Confidence: MEDIUM**

- **LDAF Resilient Food Systems Infrastructure (RFSI)** — USDA-backed, administered by LDAF, funds middle-of-supply-chain processing/storage/aggregation. Louisiana awarded **$4.6M+ in the 2024 round**. The known window closed April 2024; **monitor for future rounds**. Fits the food-oil pathway, not birdseed/deer-plot (must be human food supply chain).
- **USDA Value-Added Producer Grants (VAPG)** — up to ~$75k planning (feasibility study/business plan) and ~$250k working capital, 1:1 match (standard program terms; the specific pages didn't verify in this pass — confirm current NOFA).
- **USDA REAP** and Louisiana Economic Development rural programs are standard complements.
- A VAPG **planning grant is a natural first move**: it funds exactly the professional feasibility study this report says you need next.

**Source:** ldaf.la.gov/business/grants-funding.

## 7. Entry scenarios (rough outline — pending vendor quotes)

> Capital figures below are deliberately given as wide placeholder ranges because verified pricing does not exist yet. Break-even acreage cannot be responsibly computed until quotes and a verified oil-yield assumption are in hand.

### Scenario A — Grow + ship (lowest capital, proves the crop)
Contract acreage with a distant buyer (Red River Lubbock or an NSA-directory buyer), harvest, dry on-farm, truck out.
- **Capital:** drying/aeration upgrades only (likely low tens of thousands if existing bins adapt).
- **Economics:** ~$22–23/cwt price minus ~$3.50–4/cwt freight to Lubbock (estimated) minus drying/handling. Thin but real; act-of-God contracts limit downside.
- **Purpose:** establishes yield history, quality specs, and a buyer relationship — the foundation for B or C.

### Scenario B — Birdseed/wildlife-seed cleaning + bagging line (most credible processing entry)
Clean, screen, and bag black-oil sunflower and deer-plot seed for regional retail (feed stores, co-ops, hunting market — the Delta is prime deer country and Hancock Seed confirms a retail deer-plot seed category exists).
- **Capital:** cleaning line + bagging + building/scale — **placeholder $150k–$500k**; quote-dependent.
- **Why it leads:** simplest food-safety burden (not human food), omits the costly dehull stages, sells into a consumer market with demonstrated price tolerance, and every bag sold locally avoids the 600-mile freight penalty entirely.

### Scenario C — Elevator partnership for regional aggregation
Partner with an existing local elevator: they add sunflower handling (drying discipline, fines housekeeping, segregated bins); you (and neighbors) get a delivery point; aggregated truckloads go to a contracted buyer, or feed Scenario B.
- **Capital (to elevator):** modest — procedures, possible dryer/aeration work; the throughput cost curve says per-bushel handling cost falls fast with volume.
- **Precondition:** a signed offtake commitment from a real buyer first. Without one, the elevator carries market risk it has no reason to accept.

**Recommended sequence: A → C → B.** Prove the crop and the buyer relationship with minimal capital, aggregate through an elevator to get volume, then bring processing local once volume and margins justify the cleaning line. (Small/mid-scale oil crush is the long-game option — highest capital, FDA food-facility burden, and the refuted yield/pricing data mean it needs the most additional research.)

## 8. Open questions / next steps

1. **Get real equipment quotes** — MMCTECH and competitors, for (a) a birdseed cleaning/bagging line and (b) a small cold-press crush line; and a verified oil-yield figure for high-oleic seed in a small expeller.
2. **Call Red River Commodities (Lubbock)** and other NSA-directory birdseed packagers: will they contract Delta-grown oil sunflower, at what delivered price and quality spec? Get a real 600-mile truckload spot quote.
3. **Regulatory + insurance:** LDAF seed/feed licensing, FDA food-facility registration (only if edible oil), labeling, and insurance costs per scenario.
4. **Size the regional direct market:** deer/wildlife food-plot seed, local birdseed retail, restaurant/retail high-oleic oil — how much volume can it absorb without shipping north/west? And watch for the next **LDAF RFSI** and **USDA VAPG** application windows.

## Caveats

- All price data is time-bound (mid-October 2025 bids; 2025/26 marketing-year forecast, largely passed). Re-pull current NSA bids before deciding.
- Elevator handling costs are from 1991 and ~2016–2019 studies in nominal dollars, wheat/corn baselines.
- USDA truck rates top out at 200-mile hauls; the Lubbock estimate is an extrapolation.
- Permits, insurance, labor, confection market entry, and deer-plot seed pricing produced **no verified claims** — unresearched, not disproven.
- The elevator model's applicability where the nearest buyer is 600 miles away is an inference, not directly evidenced.
